Course Expectations

ITC-C508 · Exercise #WW-P1 · Reflections based on the required readings.

A. A Business Problem Addressed by Deep Learning-Based Predictive Models

One specific business problem that deep learning can address is customer churn (customer defection) in the mobile telecommunications industry. Churn happens when subscribers cancel their plans or transfer to competitors. Its impact is significant: as cited in the reading by Zhong and Zhuang (2018), studies by Bain & Company with Earl Sasser of Harvard Business School found that even a 5% increase in customer retention can raise profits by 25% to 95%. This means every customer lost to a competitor represents a serious blow to revenue, especially since acquiring a new customer costs far more than keeping an existing one.

Deep learning can solve this because it can manage the abundant, non-structural data that traditional analysis cannot handle. The reading describes Federico Castanedo's churn prediction model at Wise Athena, which used billions of call records from an enterprise business intelligence system to predict subscriber behavior. By training a deep neural network on historical customer data — call patterns, billing history, and service interactions — the model analyzes multiple layers of information that "can hardly be done by human work" and identifies which subscribers are likely to leave, so the company can act early with targeted retention offers instead of expensive blanket promotions.

The main challenge is the "black box" problem — deep models make accurate predictions but do not naturally explain them, which makes managers hesitant to trust or act on the results. One practical strategy appears in the reading itself: DigitalGenius's CoPilot computes a confidence level for every answer, and outputs below the threshold are routed to a human who approves or re-edits them — keeping a human in the loop for uncertain predictions. This can be combined with explainable AI (XAI) tools such as SHAP and LIME, which show which factors drove each individual prediction, and with feature-importance summaries that let decision-makers validate the model against known cases before deployment. These practices improve the transparency, interpretability, and trustworthiness of the model, turning it from a black box into a decision-support tool the business can confidently rely on.

B. Modern NLP for Intelligent, Context-Aware Conversational Systems

As Bahja's reading explains, NLP has evolved from rules-based algorithms and statistical models to machine learning, deep learning, and recurrent neural networks — a progression that produced conversational systems like Siri, Alexa, and Cortana that are "continuously updated through ML." A major breakthrough is contextual pre-training with transformer models such as Google's BERT, ULMFiT, and XLNet, which allow systems to understand language in context and even outperform humans in complex tasks like question-answering and machine translation.

What makes these systems context-aware is their ability to adapt to diverse users in real time. The reading gives concrete examples: through sentiment analysis, organizations extract insights from call-center interactions to detect customer attitudes and satisfaction levels and adjust responses accordingly. In e-governance, even citizens who are illiterate can interact with government through audio and video conversation that NLP translates into text. In education, chatbots with personalization features adapt to each learner's model, letting students negotiate with the system "using a natural conversational flow of interaction" instead of one-way e-learning content. In travel and hospitality, chatbots customize hotel and flight recommendations according to each traveler's preferences.

Modern organizations therefore deploy these NLP-driven systems as customer service assistants that answer queries in text, speech, audio, or video; as tutoring and assessment tools in education; and as opinion-mining engines that gauge public sentiment from feedback and reviews. The result is a shift from static question-and-answer bots to adaptive conversational partners that adjust to different user characteristics, communication styles, and learning needs — improving customer experience while reducing operational costs.

C. My Expectations for ITC-C508

The two required readings showed me how the concepts behind neural networks translate into real business value. From Zhong and Zhuang, I learned how feed-forward and recurrent neural networks work and how companies apply them across customer service, sales, marketing, daily operations, and risk management — from DigitalGenius's CoPilot to Salesforce's Einstein and churn prediction models built on billions of call records. From Bahja, I saw how NLP grew from simple statistical methods into transformer models like BERT that power chatbots, sentiment analysis, and intelligent tutoring across commerce, e-governance, healthcare, and education.

Because of this, I expect ITC-C508 to teach me the practical side of building these systems: preparing data, training and evaluating neural network models, applying NLP techniques like sentiment analysis, and making model predictions transparent and trustworthy rather than a black box. As a fourth-year BSIT student who built the BE@Mandaluyong city services web app, I can see how these skills would level up my own work — for example, adding an intelligent chatbot that answers residents' questions or analyzing citizen feedback with sentiment analysis, the same e-governance applications Bahja describes. Mastering these tools will support my capstone, my research, and my goal of becoming an IT professional who can build intelligent systems responsibly.
