Course Expectations

ITC-C508 · Exercise #WW-P1 · Reflections based on the required readings.

A. A Business Problem Addressed by Deep Learning-Based Predictive Models

Based on the reading by Zhong and Zhuang (2018), one business problem that deep learning can solve is customer churn in the mobile telecommunications industry. Churn means customers cancel their plans or move to a competitor. This is a serious problem because keeping a customer is much cheaper than finding a new one. The reading cited a study by Bain & Company with Earl Sasser of Harvard Business School which found that even a 5 percent increase in customer retention can raise profits by 25 to 95 percent. So every customer that leaves is a real loss for the company.

Deep learning fits this problem because it can handle huge amounts of unstructured data that people cannot analyze manually. The reading gave the example of Federico Castanedo's churn prediction model at Wise Athena, which used billions of call records from a business intelligence system to predict what subscribers will do next. When a telecom company trains a neural network on its customer data, like call patterns, billing history, and complaints, the model can spot which subscribers might leave soon. The company can then give retention offers to those specific customers before they go, instead of spending on promotions for everyone.

The weakness of deep learning is the "black box" problem. The model gives a prediction but cannot explain why, so managers find it hard to trust the results. One solution I saw in the reading is what DigitalGenius did with their CoPilot product. The system computes a confidence level for each answer, and if it is below the threshold, a human checks it first and approves or edits it. Aside from that, there are explainable AI tools like SHAP and LIME that show which factors affected each prediction, and the model can also be tested against known cases before it is deployed. With these steps, the model becomes more transparent and trustworthy, and the business can use it for real decisions with confidence.

B. Modern NLP for Intelligent, Context-Aware Conversational Systems

In the reading by Bahja, NLP started from rules-based algorithms and statistical models, then improved through machine learning, deep learning, and recurrent neural networks. This is how virtual assistants like Siri, Alexa, and Cortana became possible, and they keep getting better because they are continuously updated through machine learning. A big development is transformer models like Google's BERT, along with ULMFiT and XLNet. These models understand words based on their context, and in some tasks like question answering and machine translation they can even perform better than humans.

What makes these systems context-aware is that they adjust to different users in real time. The reading gave several examples. Companies use sentiment analysis on call center conversations to know if a customer is satisfied or upset, then respond in the right way. In e-governance, citizens who cannot read or write can still communicate with the government through voice or video, and NLP converts it to text. In education, chatbots adapt to each student's level and let the learner interact through natural conversation instead of just watching one-way videos or modules. In travel, chatbots suggest hotels and flights based on the traveler's own preferences.

Because of this, organizations now use NLP systems as customer service assistants that can reply through text, speech, audio, or video, as tutors and assessment tools in schools, and as tools to check public opinion from reviews and feedback. Chatbots are no longer fixed question and answer machines. They adapt to the user's style and needs, which improves the customer experience and lowers the cost of operations at the same time.

C. My Expectations for ITC-C508

The two readings showed me how neural networks are actually used in business. From Zhong and Zhuang, I learned the basics of feed-forward and recurrent neural networks and saw real examples in customer service, sales, marketing, daily operations, and risk management, like DigitalGenius CoPilot, Salesforce Einstein, and the churn prediction model built from billions of call records. From Bahja, I learned how NLP developed into transformer models like BERT, and how it powers chatbots, sentiment analysis, and tutoring systems in commerce, e-governance, healthcare, and education.

Because of these readings, I expect ITC-C508 to teach me the hands-on side of these technologies: preparing data, training and testing models, using NLP techniques like sentiment analysis, and making predictions explainable instead of a black box. I built a web app called Be@Mandaluyong, a tourist trail and guide for Mandaluyong City, and I can already imagine applying these lessons to it, like adding a chatbot that answers visitors' questions or running sentiment analysis on user feedback, similar to the examples in the readings. Learning these skills will help me in my capstone and in my goal to work in the IT industry and build intelligent systems responsibly.
